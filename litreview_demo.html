Research workflow

How I use AI for literature review

A four-phase process where human judgment drives every step and AI handles synthesis across many papers at once. Written by Yalda Daryani, CDT Research Intern.

Before reading Organising papers in Notion

Before I read anything, I organise all papers into a Notion database. Each paper is a row. The database is structured around big topics (broad theoretical territories) and subtopics (specific mechanisms or phenomena within them). This forces me to situate a paper intellectually before I engage with it, and makes synthesis far easier later.

A big topic like Cultural Evolution contains subtopics such as Language evolution, Norm transmission, or Risk-taking across generations. Every paper is tagged to at least one subtopic, which rolls up to its parent big topic. Papers can appear under multiple big topics.

Phase 1 Deep reading (human only)

↓
Phase 2 Purpose-driven tailoring

The template flexes based on why I am reading. I shift where I place emphasis so notes stay purposeful, not encyclopedic.

Skim intro. Focus heavily on methods section: design choices, assumptions, implementation details, parameter settings. Note what would need to change to apply this method to a new domain.
↓
Phase 3 AI synthesis: Claude enters here

Once notes are complete across several papers, I paste them into Claude and ask for synthesis. Claude works from my notes rather than the papers directly, so the intellectual judgment stays mine.

I always synthesise within a subtopic first, then across subtopics within a big topic. This keeps the synthesis grounded and prevents Claude from flattening genuinely distinct literatures into one vague summary.

Core synthesis prompt
Below are my structured notes from 4 papers on computational models of identity, opinion dynamics, and social learning. Each entry follows the same template. Synthesize across these notes. Do not summarize each paper individually. Identify: 1. The 2-3 dominant themes running through this literature 2. Key tensions or contradictions between papers 3. Methodological patterns or gaps 4. Open questions this literature leaves unresolved Write in clear analytical prose. Flag genuine conflicts. Do not flatten disagreements.

Prompt library

Beyond the core synthesis, these are the prompts I use most often at other moments in the review process. Expand any to copy.

Gap analysis within a subtopic Gaps
Here are my notes from [N] papers tagged under [subtopic name]. I want to understand what this cluster of work has NOT addressed. Identify: 1. Populations, contexts, or mechanisms that appear only as assumptions or future work 2. Methodological blind spots shared across papers 3. Questions that multiple papers gesture toward but none fully address 4. Anything I should be reading that seems conspicuously absent Be specific. Vague gaps ("more research needed") are not useful.
Connecting two subtopics Cross-topic
I have notes from two subtopics within [big topic name]: Subtopic A: [subtopic A name] [paste notes] Subtopic B: [subtopic B name] [paste notes] What does each body of work assume that the other could challenge? Where do they share unexplored common ground? Are there papers in one set whose methods or findings would directly change how I read the other? Write as a researcher deciding whether to merge or keep these two threads separate in a literature review.
Pressure-testing a single paper Deep dive
Here are my notes on one paper: [paste single paper note] I want to stress-test this paper's contribution. Given what I have told you: 1. What is the strongest version of the argument it makes? 2. What would a skeptical reviewer flag? 3. What would need to be true for its main finding to fail to generalize? 4. What does it contribute methodologically vs. substantively? Do not be charitable for the sake of it. If the limitations undermine the claims, say so.
Draft a literature review paragraph Writing
Here are my synthesis notes on [subtopic name]. Draft one literature review paragraph for an academic audience. The paragraph should: - Open with the core theoretical claim or puzzle the literature addresses - Integrate at least 3 papers as evidence, not as separate summaries - Name at least one unresolved tension - End with a sentence that opens toward my own research question: [insert your RQ here] Write in confident academic prose. Use hedging only where the evidence genuinely requires it. Do not use bullet points or headers.
Why this works: AI handles pattern recognition across many notes simultaneously, something humans do slowly and with cognitive fatigue. But the notes encode my judgment: what mattered, what was weak, why I was reading each paper. Claude's synthesis reflects my intellectual frame, not a generic summarization of abstracts.

Tips for your group

Things that took me a while to learn and would have saved time earlier.

Tag before you read Assigning big topic and subtopic tags before opening a paper forces you to place it intellectually first. It also prevents your Notion database from becoming unsearchable.
Synthesise within subtopics first Never paste papers from different big topics into a single synthesis prompt. The output will be superficial. Go subtopic by subtopic, then combine upward.
Your notes are the bottleneck The quality of AI synthesis is entirely bounded by note quality. Thin notes produce thin synthesis. The Notion template is not optional; it is the system.
Status column saves hours Keeping a Status field (Unread / In progress / Done / Revisit) lets you filter at a glance and prevents re-reading papers you have already processed.
Ask for contradictions explicitly Claude defaults to finding agreement. If you want genuine tensions surfaced, say so in the prompt: "Flag genuine conflicts. Do not flatten disagreements."
Keep one relevance sentence honest The "Relevance to me" field should say why you are reading this paper relative to your specific research question, not just what the paper is about. This is what makes synthesis feel like yours.
