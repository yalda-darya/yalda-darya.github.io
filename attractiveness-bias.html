A Contest Study to Reduce Attractiveness-Based Discrimination in Social Judgment

Leveraging Research Contests to Identify Strategies for Fairer Decision-Making

Full Paper

Overview

Discrimination based on irrelevant social cues like physical attractiveness can shape hiring, promotion, and academic outcomes. Yet, effective and scalable solutions remain limited.This large-scale research contest brought together 30 interventions from global teams, tested with 20,000+ participants, to evaluate strategies for reducing attractiveness-based discrimination in social judgment. I joined this project during the first years of my Master’s at the University of Tehran, contributing as a collaborator to this multi-lab effort. Using a signal detection theory framework, we identified interventions that reduced both decision bias (favoring attractive applicants) and decision noise (errors in judgment). Importantly, the most effective strategies guided decision-makers’ attention toward objective criteria, providing practical tools for real-world organizational decision-making.

Key Contributions

  • Designed a Bias-Reduction Intervention: Applied insights from moral psychology, recognizing that biases often stem from moral intuitions, to create an intervention that reduced attractiveness-based discrimination in evaluation tasks.
  • Focused on Hiring-Relevant Decisions: My intervention redirected attention away from appearance and toward job-relevant criteria, offering practical strategies for improving fairness in hiring and promotion processes.
  • Large-Scale Experimental Validation: Contributed to a global contest study with 30 interventions tested on 20,000+ participants, ensuring rigorous, comparative evidence for what works in reducing bias.
  • Organizational Relevance:Helped identify scalable interventions that can generalize across contexts, informing how organizations structure evaluation systems to minimize bias and improve decision quality.

Methodology Timeline

Benchmark Phase (Study 1, N = 11,000+)

Initial intervention testing with online participants to benchmark effectivenes.

eplication Phase (Study 2, N = 4,400)

Direct replication with paid participants (Prolific) to ensure robustness.

Generalization Phase (Study 3, N = 3,500)

Tested generalizability across two discrimination domains such as attractiveness and political affiliation.

Applied Phase (Study 4, N = 1,200)

Extended to participants with hiring experience, confirming organizational relevance.

Results and Implications

  • Effective Interventions Identified: Four interventions reliably reduced discrimination, with two reducing both noise and bias.
  • Scalable Solutions: Interventions were short (<7 minutes), easily deployable, and avoided explicit mention of attractiveness, enhancing transferability.
  • Applied Relevance: Findings inform how organizations can structure evaluation processes (e.g., clear criteria, accountability prompts) to reduce bias in hiring and promotion.
