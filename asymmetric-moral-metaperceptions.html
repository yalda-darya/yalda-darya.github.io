Asymmetric Moral Metaperceptions: Consequences for Political Polarization

Investigating how misperceptions of opponents' moral values sustain political division and testing corrective interventions.

Full Paper (PDF) GitHub Code (WIP)

Overview

This research identifies moral metaperceptions [cite: 16, 79, 1073][cite_start]—beliefs about how ideological opponents evaluate one's own moral values—as a critical, overlooked factor fueling political polarization[cite: 16, 79, 1073]. [cite_start]We demonstrate that the political divide is sustained not only by what people believe, but also by what they "think others believe about them"[cite: 15]. [cite_start]Across four studies, we establish a robust pattern of "asymmetric inaccuracy" and show that correcting these misperceptions can significantly increase trust and reduce perceived threat between political groups[cite: 20, 22, 1081].

Key Contributions

    [cite_start]
  • Discovery of Asymmetric Moral Bias: We found that liberals consistently underestimate how positively conservatives view their binding values (Loyalty, Authority, Purity), while conservatives tend to overestimate how positively liberals view their individualizing values (Care, Fairness)[cite: 20, 1078].
    • [cite_start]
  • Multi-Method Validation: We combined Natural Language Processing (NLP) on an $\sim$11 million tweet corpus (Study 1) with three large-scale, pre-registered experimental studies (Studies 2-4) to establish a comprehensive picture of this psychological mechanism[cite: 92, 1074].
    • [cite_start]
  • Predictive Power of Mismatches: We showed that these metaperceptual errors are robust predictors of reduced intergroup trust and heightened perceived threat across polarized issues like abortion and gun control[cite: 88, 777, 785].
    • [cite_start]
  • Informational Intervention: We demonstrated that a simple, informational feedback intervention—telling participants the outgroup rated them "better than expected"—is a scalable method to increase trust and reduce threat between partisans[cite: 889, 1046, 1071].

Methodology Timeline (4 Studies)

Study 1: Identifying Moral Framing in Twitter Discourse (Computational)

[cite_start]Analyzed 10.9 million tweets on the abortion debate to confirm that moral language use differs when discussing ingroup stance versus outgroup positions[cite: 131, 414]. [cite_start]Employed fine-tuned BERTweet for stance classification (Macro $F1=0.758$) [cite: 142] [cite_start]and RoBERTa for moral foundation tagging [cite: 144][cite_start], establishing the foundational role of morality in the conflict[cite: 403].

Study 2 & 3: Measuring Asymmetric Misperceptions (Experimental)

[cite_start]Used a 2 (Group) x 3 (Condition: Ingroup, Outgroup, Metaperception) design to measure moral and social (Warmth, Competence, Social Distance) perceptions across the abortion and gun control issues[cite: 448, 677]. [cite_start]Quantified the mismatch between outgroup's actual rating and ingroup's predicted rating, confirming the asymmetric bias across different political contexts[cite: 627, 853].

Study 4: Testing Corrective Feedback (Intervention)

[cite_start]Conducted a $N > 1,200$ experiment where participants received false but plausible feedback (Better-Than-Expected, Worse-Than-Expected, Control) on how the outgroup morally evaluated them[cite: 895, 912, 913]. [cite_start]Measured subsequent changes in intergroup trust and perceived threat as the primary outcomes[cite: 920, 921].

Results and Implications

[cite_start]The results provide clear evidence that moral metaperceptions are malleable and act as a leverage point for depolarization[cite: 1080, 1082].

    [cite_start]
  • Correction Works: Better-than-expected feedback significantly increased trust and reduced threat compared to worse-than-expected feedback across both ideological groups[cite: 953, 1000, 1046].
    • [cite_start]
  • Empathy Moderates: The positive effects of the feedback intervention were strongest among pro-life individuals higher in empathy, who showed greater increases in trust and larger reductions in threat when receiving positive information[cite: 1005, 1033].
    • [cite_start]
  • Policy Implication: The findings suggest that interventions to reduce polarization should be tailored to address specific moral misperceptions for each group (e.g., liberal pessimism vs. conservative optimism), possibly by combining empathy-building with metaperceptual corrections[cite: 1088, 1091, 1092].
