Measuring the semantic priming effect across many languages

Standardizing Decision Tasks Across Languages for Global Data Collection

Full Paper

Overview

Semantic priming—the phenomenon where people respond faster to a word when it is preceded by a related word (e.g., dog–cat)— is not just central to psycholinguistics and cognitive science. It also plays a critical role in how people process information in everyday life, from reading and memory to decision-making and human-computer interaction. Priming effects shape how quickly we interpret search results, how effectively we navigate digital interfaces, and even how advertising or political messages influence our judgments.

Because of its broad importance, understanding semantic priming at scale provides valuable insights for fields like natural language processing, user experience research, marketing, and behavioral data science. Yet, most previous research has been limited to English and small samples, making it difficult to know how robust or universal these effects really are. Without a global dataset, applications in AI, cross-cultural communication, and product design risk being biased toward Western language patterns.

This project filled that gap. As part of a multi-lab collaboration under the Psychological Science Accelerator, we collected data from 25,000+ participants in 19 languages. By standardizing stimuli, timing, and decision tasks, we created the largest cross-linguistic dataset of semantic priming effects to date. This resource not only advances science but also offers a foundation for more culturally inclusive models of language and behavior that are directly relevant to real-world applications.

Key Contributions

  • Experimental Infrastructure: Created a JSON-based experimental file defining stimuli, timing, and decision options. This standardized process allowed all labs to run the study consistently and efficiently.
  • Stimuli Development: Collaborated on building word-pair stimuli for the priming task across multiple languages, ensuring cross-linguistic comparability.
  • Data Collection: Assisted in running the large-scale lexical decision task across diverse participant samples.
  • Analysis Planning: Developed analysis approaches to evaluate cross-linguistic variation and ensure the reliability of priming effects.
  • Manuscript Writing: Contributed to drafting and refining sections of the collaborative paper, highlighting methodological innovations and implications.

Results and Implications

Robust semantic priming effects were observed across all tested languages, confirming the generalizability of this fundamental cognitive phenomenon. At the same time, the study highlighted cross-linguistic variability, suggesting that cultural and linguistic contexts shape the strength of priming effects.
